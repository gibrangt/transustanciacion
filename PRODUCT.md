# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Catholics engaged in apologetic debates with Evangelicals who want solid, well-sourced evidence that patristic tradition supports Transubstantiation. They arrive with a prior conviction but need the evidence organized, cited, and presented in a form credible enough to use in real arguments. Secondary audience: intellectually serious seekers who want to understand what the early Church actually said.

## Product Purpose

An editorial apologetic site presenting the debate between Dr. Dante Urbina (Catholic) and Mr. Edgar Pacheco (Evangelical) on whether the Church Fathers support Transubstantiation. The site has a clear thesis — Urbina's arguments are better substantiated — and proves it by presenting Pacheco's objections with full fidelity before responding. Success: a Catholic reader leaves with organized, citable evidence; a skeptical reader leaves persuaded that the Catholic reading of the Fathers is at minimum the more defensible one.

## Brand Personality

Passionate · Learned · Fair

The voice is confident without being combative, rigorous without being cold. It cares deeply about the truth of the question, not just winning the argument. The tone is that of a scholar who has spent years with primary sources and can afford to be generous with opposing views precisely because the evidence is strong.

## Anti-references

- **Generic Catholic parish site**: stock-photo crosses, gold filigree ornamental borders, everything in Times New Roman, clip-art iconography. Looks devotional, not scholarly — undermines the credibility the content demands.
- **Protestant apologetics blog**: busy sidebars, aggressive polemical tone, mid-2000s forum aesthetic. Looks combative rather than persuasive.
- **Modern SaaS / startup UI**: glassmorphism, gradient-text headings, hero metric cards, identical card grids. Tonally wrong for a theological document; reads as decoration over substance.

## Design Principles

1. **Evidence before assertion** — every claim earns its place through quoted primary sources. The design should make the citation hierarchy legible at a glance; readers must be able to distinguish claim from evidence from rebuttal.
2. **Fair witness** — the opposing arguments are presented with full fidelity. The layout gives Pacheco's position the same visual dignity as Urbina's before answering it. Credibility depends on perceived honesty.
3. **Scholarly warmth** — academic rigor without institutional coldness. The reader should feel the weight of two millennia of theological tradition, not stare at a Wikipedia article.
4. **Typography as argument** — no decorative stock photography; the text hierarchy IS the design. What gets set large, what gets set in italics, what gets set in monospace — these are editorial choices that carry the argument.
5. **Catholic confidence** — the site has a thesis and does not hedge. The visual language should match: clear hierarchy, no equivocal waffling in the layout, the thesis stated plainly at the top.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA. Body text ≥ 4.5:1 contrast against background. Large text / headings ≥ 3:1. Keyboard navigation required (`:target` panels, `<details>` summaries). `prefers-reduced-motion` respected throughout. `lang="es"` on `<html>`. ARIA labels on all interactive elements.
